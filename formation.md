# Formation

## Why agentic work surfaced this

Agents reason associatively — making connections across their context in ways that don't follow linear paths. Working with that tendency — layering on additional agents, each entering deeply into a limited context, letting findings accumulate and cross-pollinate at the orchestration level — produces better output through more thorough coverage, richer cross-referencing, and synthesis the human couldn't do alone at the same speed.

The [method](method.md) emerged from recognizing this pattern and building a workflow around it. This document is about the background behind that recognition.

## What the method taught back

Early in a large architecture investigation, the orchestrating agent consistently flagged certain questions as "needs human judgment." Domain boundary questions — things that looked like they required insider knowledge. But the questions didn't have the texture of things that genuinely require human judgment. I could see that the data and tooling available to the agent were sufficient to resolve them — the agent was anchored to what was local to it, not reaching for what its tools could actually answer.

I pushed back on each one. Every time, directed investigation resolved the question from data.

This recalibrated my understanding of the human role. Any experienced user of agentic tools will discover some version of this — the agent's locality bias is visible to anyone paying attention. The recognition shaped how I think about the human role: noticing where the agent is limited by its local context and directing it past that boundary.

The exchange works in the other direction too. Sometimes I'll have a felt sense that something is off — a draft that reads wrong, an assumption that doesn't sit right — and I'll ask the agent to articulate what the problem might be. The agent's attempt, even when it misses, gives the felt sense something to push against. The articulation often crystallizes through the process of correcting the agent's framing, producing a sharper distinction than either of us started with. This is productive friction — the exchange itself generating clarity.

And sometimes I'll explicitly invite the agent to push back on my own thinking — to counter an intuition I have, or to find the weakness in a direction I'm leaning. The friction is bidirectional by design. The human role includes knowing when to open that space.

Each pass through this kind of exchange narrows the space of misunderstanding. The method refines through correction — what something isn't becomes clearer, which sharpens what it is. Understanding accumulates as much through distinguishing as through adding.

## The background

My earlier training was in a field that rewarded the opposite of decomposition — entering perspectives fully, holding contradictions without resolving them, thinking associatively across domains, and letting structure emerge from sustained attention rather than imposing it from outside. The work was in the ongoing engagement across perspectives, not in reaching conclusions.

These capacities turned out to be relevant to the agentic work — though I'm aware that the same training that produced them also produces the pattern-recognition that finds the connections. Entering deeply into a limited perspective and then switching to another is what each agent does within its context window; integrating across those perspectives without collapsing them is the orchestrator's job. The associative attention — noticing when unrelated findings start rhyming — is what makes the research phase productive. The comfort with contradiction is what kept me from forcing premature synthesis.

### A second language

Learning to decompose problems as an engineer was a genuine cognitive shift — holding a problem as discrete, ordered steps rather than a rich, interconnected whole. Once it arrived, it didn't replace the earlier mode. It gave me a second one, and the flow between sitting with ambiguity and breaking problems down became where a lot of the work happened.

### The synthesis

Two modes. The combination produced the method. The inductive attention — associative accumulation, perspective-entry, pattern recognition — drives the research and review phases. The deductive scaffolding — decomposition, sequencing, formal verification — structures the planning and implementation phases. Both loop into each other constantly; modes shift within phases, sometimes within a single exchange.

The pattern that runs through the whole [method](method.md) is: recognition (inductive), then structuring (deductive), then [implementing](workflows/implementation.md). The practice came first, built up over months of agentic work. The patterns became visible through reflection.
