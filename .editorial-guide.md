# Editorial Guide

> Not for publication. Cross-session editorial reference.

## Voice and Tone

- Practitioner-facing. Direct, not academic. Show through practice, not argument.
- Confident without prescriptive. "This is how it works" not "you should do this."
- No filler, hedging, or throat-clearing.
- **Don't talk down.** Explaining is fine; explaining *at* someone isn't. "Let me tell you what you do, then what I do instead" = smarmy. Restructure.
- **Alternatives, not corrections.** Present the method on its own terms. Don't contrast against the reader's presumed default.
- **No superior positioning.** Doesn't matter if the approach is better. Doesn't need to sound that way.
- **Eliminate "it's not A, it's B."** Agentic writing tic. Drop the "not A" part; integrate the B.
- **Watch for triadic lists.** Agents default to three-item lists ("A, B, and C"). One or two per section is fine; when every paragraph has one, the pattern becomes conspicuous and the writing feels mechanical. Vary sentence structure. Sometimes two items or four are more accurate than three. Also watch for triads that close with "or some combination" — this frames the triad as a menu to pick from, which limits rather than illustrates.
- **Pin down "felt sense" where possible.** Use "felt" only where pre-articulability is genuinely the point (gap recognition before you can name the gap). Elsewhere, be concrete: inconsistent evidence, gaps in coverage, patterns that don't match mental model. "Felt" is earned in one or two places; overused, it sounds vague.
- **Method voice:** method.md addresses practitioners but avoid forced "you." Restructure to remove pronouns where possible; keep "you" only where it's genuinely natural practitioner address. Prefer describing how the method works over instructing the reader.

## Pronouns

- **"I"** default for personal experience/actions.
- **"You"** sparingly for universal moments or practices the reader might try. Watch for disguised "I."
- **"One"** works in two registers: aphoristic statements with elevated language, and descriptions of traditions with inherent gravitas. Single-use only — collapses with repetition. Useful when "you" implies shared experience the reader doesn't have.
- **Mixing** is fine when shifts are motivated (I = specific experience, you = invitation to reader).

## Hameivin Yavin

References appear as naming choices and framing, never as explanation. Docs work fully for readers who catch zero resonances.

- "Formation" works. Don't explain why.
- Terms should be precise in plain English AND resonant for the knowledgeable.
- If a reference needs explanation to land, it's doing the wrong work.
- Test: does a senior engineer with no humanities background get full value?
- **Watch for signposts vs. breadcrumbs.** "Living commentary" + "annotated, layered, associative" makes the source too legible. Find language that carries meaning without specificity.

## Structure

- **Foundational first.** Earlier docs inform later ones.
- **Integrate, don't append.**
- **Repetition acceptable** when it reinforces. Don't sacrifice readability for DRY.
- **Cross-reference, don't duplicate.** Restating a principle with workflow-specific texture is fine.
- **Charts/diagrams independently comprehensible.** Don't split across docs.
- **Each doc self-sufficient for its scope.**

## What Belongs Where

- **method.md**: Building blocks across all workflows. Phases, knowledge state, convergence, collaboration model, agent associative strengths.
- **workflows/**: How building blocks compose for specific artifact types. Concrete. References method for why.
- **formation.md (Background)**: How the method developed. Nested under Method in nav. Brief — anecdote, productive friction, one paragraph of background. Not required reading.
- **quick-start.md**: Actionable techniques extracted from the collection. Concrete, no theory. Links back to full treatments. Alternative entry point for time-crunched readers.
- **agent-patterns.md**: Observed agent behavioral patterns — what they do, why, how the method works with them. Reference doc, not required reading. Complements method.md (method says what to do; patterns says why the behavior exists).
- **open-questions.md**: Living edges. Keeps other docs clean of hedging.

## Formation-Specific

- **Lead with practice, not biography.** Personal details come later, after the mode of thinking is established.
- **Earn the personal.** More specific/personal = later in the doc.
- **Avoid formalism.** Lived-in, not observed from outside.
- **Don't protest.** "This isn't mystical" = defensive. If you feel the need, the problem is upstream.
- **Cross-linking.** Start anywhere, end up everywhere.

## Calibrating Formation Claims

- **Don't attribute common expert practice to the formation.** Convergence-before-verification, gap recognition, batch feedback — these are how experienced practitioners work regardless of background. The method makes them explicit; the formation didn't invent them.
- **Be honest about what you can't separate.** When an insight might be formation or might be tool familiarity, say so. "I can't fully separate whether this was the formation or just experience" is stronger than claiming either.
- **The genuinely distinctive elements** (comfort with sustained ambiguity, full perspective-entry, the structural parallel between lens-entering and agent orchestration) deserve emphasis. These are what the background assessor couldn't attribute to common practice.
- **The self-fulfilling risk is real.** The formation trains exactly the kind of pattern-recognition that finds connections to the formation. Guard against retroactive coherence.
- **Method stands alone.** It should be usable by anyone regardless of background. Formation illuminates but isn't prerequisite.

## Accumulated Feedback

- Hevruta metaphor overreaches for the actual dynamic. Use carefully or not at all.
- Method should be legible as practical, not epistemological.
- "Fractal" — state once, show through practice.
- "You drive quality; Claude drives throughput" is principles-level.
- Avoid new-agey tone.
- Don't make findings sound like punchlines.
- Formation closing: the actual insight is that agentic work brought the earlier formation forward.
- Many agentic workflows fight non-determinism. This method works with it. Worth calling out where relevant.

## Audience

- **Primary (method + workflows):** Technical practitioners using agentic tools. Senior. Not necessarily interested in epistemology.
- **Secondary (formation):** Practitioners wanting the why. Cross-disciplinary backgrounds. The operator, for clarity.
