## Voice (universal)

These apply to every page on the site.

- **No performance.** Don't signal importance, insight, urgency, or reaction. The content carries its own weight. "I found that surprising" tells the reader what to feel; the observation itself is the surprise.
- Practitioner-facing. Direct, not academic. Show through practice, not argument.
- Confident without prescriptive. "This is how it works" not "you should do this."
- No filler, hedging, or throat-clearing.
- **Register matches concept.** Elevated language is fine when it's precise and the concept needs it. The voice moves between casual and technical registers naturally — reaching for the word that fits, then grounding it in plain language nearby. The rule is against inflation — a word that sounds bigger than the idea it's carrying — not against conceptual precision.
- **Don't talk down.** Explaining is fine; explaining *at* someone isn't.
- **Present on its own terms.** No contrast against the reader's presumed default, no superior positioning, no "it's not A, it's B" (agentic tic — drop the "not A" part, integrate the B).

## Voice by doc type

### Method, reference, quick start

Impersonal, declarative. The framework exists independently of the author. Avoid "I" and forced "you." Restructure to remove pronouns where possible; keep "you" only where it's genuinely natural practitioner address.

### Articles

The author is present and thinking in text. "I" is natural and expected.

- **Think in front of the reader.** Show the thought forming, not just the conclusion.
- **Concrete before abstract.** Lead with what happened or what was observed. Let the principle arrive from the specifics.
- **Comfortable incomplete.** Not every thread resolves. Note it, leave it, move on. Forcing closure reads as artificial.
- **Write what you know, not what agents found.** Research agents produce inventories. The article contains the author's observations and understanding. Don't cite for credibility — cite for precision. If you can't speak to a specific source yourself, it doesn't belong. The observations are the proof; don't establish that you did research before sharing what you found.
- **No announcing, no performed posture.** Don't frame the act of writing ("I want to articulate," "it's worth exploring") or declare epistemic stance upfront ("this is one perspective"). The reader is already reading. Uncertainty comes through inline: "I think," "I've found," unresolved open questions. Don't smooth uncertainty into false confidence, but don't perform humility either.
- **Drafting, not finishing.** When generating article content, the agent is drafting for the author's voice. Flag where you're uncertain rather than filling in confidently. The author will vet.
- **Raw voice in blockquotes.** The author's unfiltered writing (a message, a note, a fragment of thinking) can appear in blockquotes anywhere in an article. Blockquotes are not edited. When reviewing or drafting, proactively suggest where the author's own voice would fit well in the structure, either replacing an expanded section or as an addition. The author can write something new as needed.

### Background

"I" — reflective, brief. Follows article voice with specific constraints: four paragraphs, anecdote-led, hameivin yavin intact.

## Plain language

- **Inflation is register outpacing concept.** "Operational structure," "become legible," "primary deliverable is understanding" — these sound bigger than the ideas they carry. "Qualitative attention," "collaborative friction," "temporal locality bias" — these are precise terms doing real work. The test: does a plainer word lose meaning, or just lose weight?
- **Don't inflate.** No significance claims, no self-important framing, no redundant self-positioning. If the content demonstrates the point, the claim is redundant. Cut it.
- **No presuming the reader's response.** Let the reader draw their own conclusions.
- **No unverifiable absolutes.** "No standard addresses X," "nobody has done Y," "aren't captured anywhere" — these overstate what the author can know. Prefer cutting the claim entirely and letting the observation stand. "I haven't found" is just the same pattern in first person.
- **No oxford commas.** Drop the comma before "and" in lists.
- **One idea per paragraph.** Split at facet boundaries. A longer paragraph on one coherent idea is fine; a short paragraph packing two unrelated ideas is the problem.
- **Test:** Would a busy practitioner skim past this, or find it annoying? If yes, tighten.

## Agentic writing tics

Patterns agents produce that need catching. The [voice samples](.voice-samples.md) show the author's natural rhythm. When agentic tics dominate, that rhythm flattens into repeating patterns. The fix is restoring the natural flow, not mechanical variation.

- **Triadic lists.** Agents default to groups of three. Sometimes two or four are more accurate.
- **Contrast constructions.** "It's not A, it's B." Drop the "not A" part; integrate the B directly.
- **Em dash overuse.** Agents lean on em dashes for parenthetical asides, mid-sentence pivots, and list introductions. A few do real work. When every other sentence has one, the rhythm is the same sentence over and over. Most can become commas, periods, or restructured sentences.
- **Pin down "felt sense."** Use "felt" only where pre-articulability is genuinely the point. Elsewhere, be concrete.
- **Don't "fix" comma splices or parenthetical asides.** "That takes practice, experience" and "(into actionable steps)" are the author's voice. Comma splices serve rhythm. Parentheses are lighter than em dashes for inline qualification.
- **Summative verdicts.** "The tradeoff is real." "That's the clearest gain." Agents stamp clean conclusions where the author would leave the observation standing or lean into one side. The author's evaluative mode is messier — "I would say this is pretty good," "good enough for me as is."
- **Syntactic repetition across consecutive sentences.** Three sentences in a row doing the same grammatical move (origin-tracing, cause-stating, qualifying) flatten the rhythm even when each sentence is fine individually. Vary the structure.

## Readability

- **Two-sentence bullet rule.** Bold lead-in + 1-2 sentences. Push elaboration to sub-bullets or follow-on paragraph. In articles, enumerations can run as prose when items aren't parallel enough for clean bullets.
- **Bold lead-ins for scannable sections.**
- **Callouts sparingly.** 2-3 per page max.
- **Break after diagrams.**
- **Definition lists over inline definitions.**

## Hameivin yavin

References appear as naming choices and framing, never as explanation. Docs work fully for readers who catch zero resonances.

- Terms should be precise in plain English AND resonant for the knowledgeable.
- If a reference needs explanation to land, it's doing the wrong work.
- Test: does a senior engineer with no humanities background get full value?

## Audience

- **Method, workflows, quick start:** Technical practitioners using agentic tools. Senior.
- **Background:** Practitioners wanting the why. Cross-disciplinary backgrounds.
- **Articles:** Same primary audience.

## Structure

- **Foundational first.** Earlier docs inform later ones.
- **Integrate, don't append.**
- **Cross-reference, don't duplicate.** Restating a principle with workflow-specific texture is fine. Verbatim repetition is not.
- **Each doc self-sufficient for its scope.**
- **Workflows are reference material.** Lead with what's unique, not a recap of the method.

## What belongs where

- **method.md**: Phases (including implementation), knowledge state, convergence, collaboration model, how agents reason.
- **formation.md (Background)**: How the method developed. Brief. Not required reading.
- **quick-start.md**: Actionable techniques. No theory. Links to full treatments.
- **agent-patterns.md**: Observed behavioral patterns. Reference doc.
- **workflows/**: How building blocks compose for specific work types. Lead with what's unique.
- **articles/**: Standalone pieces. Can reference the method but don't depend on it.
- **about.md**: Brief, warm, author present. Not a resume — LinkedIn handles career details. Avoid content that dates (current role, current focus).
- **open-questions.md**: Living edges. Not published.

## Background-specific

- **Brief.** Four paragraphs. Anecdote, productive friction, background, closing.
- **Lead with practice.** The locality bias anecdote is the opening.
- **Hameivin yavin intact.** "A field that rewarded the opposite of decomposition" — the unnamed tradition.

## Calibrating claims

- **Don't attribute common expert practice to the background.** Convergence-before-verification, gap recognition, batch feedback — experienced practitioners do these regardless.
- **Be honest about what you can't separate.**
- **Method stands alone.** The background illuminates but isn't prerequisite.

## Employer IP

No employer-specific content reaches the site, the commit history, or any published artifact. This includes the author's blockquotes.

- Describe the class of problem and category of solution, not the specific implementation.
- Generalize tool names, architecture details, internal processes, team structures, customer details, product features.
- When uncertain whether something is proprietary, flag it.
