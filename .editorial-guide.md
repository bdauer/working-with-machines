# Editorial Guide

> Not for publication. Cross-session editorial reference.

## Voice and Tone

- Practitioner-facing. Direct, not academic. Show through practice, not argument.
- Confident without prescriptive. "This is how it works" not "you should do this."
- No filler, hedging, or throat-clearing.
- **Don't talk down.** Explaining is fine; explaining *at* someone isn't. "Let me tell you what you do, then what I do instead" = smarmy. Restructure.
- **Alternatives, not corrections.** Present the method on its own terms. Don't contrast against the reader's presumed default.
- **No superior positioning.** Doesn't matter if the approach is better. Doesn't need to sound that way.
- **Eliminate "it's not A, it's B."** Agentic writing tic. Drop the "not A" part; integrate the B.
- **Watch for triadic lists.** Agents default to three-item lists. Vary structure. Sometimes two or four are more accurate than three.
- **Pin down "felt sense" where possible.** Use "felt" only where pre-articulability is genuinely the point. Elsewhere, be concrete.

## Plain Language

- **No register inflation.** "Qualitative attention" is earned if it does real work. "Operational structure," "become legible," "primary deliverable is understanding," "recalibrates" — use plain words. "Structure," "emerge," "the goal is to understand something," "changes."
- **No significance claims.** "A significant part of the work." "Matters most." "The most valuable findings." If the surrounding text demonstrates the point, the claim is redundant. Cut it.
- **No self-importance in framing.** "Emerged from practice." "A reference for practitioners." "This is a design principle." "Implementation is a research activity." Language that signals significance rather than demonstrating it. The content should carry its own weight.
- **No redundant self-positioning.** Don't say the same thing twice in different framing. "Recalibrated my understanding" then "shaped how I think" — same thing. Pick one.
- **No presuming the reader's response.** "Experienced practitioners already do this." "Start here if you want to change how you work." "Anyone paying attention will notice." Let the reader draw their own conclusions.
- **One idea per paragraph.** Web readers scan. 2-3 sentences per paragraph. When a paragraph covers multiple facets, split at the facet boundary.
- **Test:** Would a busy practitioner skim past this, or find it annoying? If yes, tighten.

## Readability

- **Two-sentence bullet rule.** When a bullet list does structural work (Human Role, Knowledge Accumulation, Agent Patterns), keep each bullet to a bold lead-in + 1-2 sentences. Push elaboration to sub-bullets or a follow-on paragraph.
- **Bold lead-ins for scannable sections.** Every bullet in a conceptual list should have a bolded term or phrase that works as a scan target.
- **Callouts sparingly.** `{: .note }` and `{: .important }` for standalone principles worth remembering independently. 2-3 per page max.
- **Break after diagrams.** Give diagrams their own visual moment before the next paragraph.
- **Definition lists over inline definitions.** When a paragraph introduces two or more terms with parenthetical definitions, convert to bullets.

## Pronouns

- **"I"** default for personal experience/actions (background page only).
- **"You"** sparingly for universal moments or practices the reader might try. Watch for disguised "I."
- **Method voice:** avoid forced "you." Restructure to remove pronouns where possible; keep "you" only where it's genuinely natural practitioner address.

## Hameivin Yavin

References appear as naming choices and framing, never as explanation. Docs work fully for readers who catch zero resonances.

- Terms should be precise in plain English AND resonant for the knowledgeable.
- If a reference needs explanation to land, it's doing the wrong work.
- Test: does a senior engineer with no humanities background get full value?

## Structure

- **Foundational first.** Earlier docs inform later ones.
- **Integrate, don't append.**
- **Cross-reference, don't duplicate.** Restating a principle with workflow-specific texture is fine. Verbatim repetition across docs is not.
- **Charts/diagrams independently comprehensible.**
- **Each doc self-sufficient for its scope.**
- **Workflows are reference material.** Not required reading after method.md. Each should lead with what's unique to that workflow, not recap the method.

## What Belongs Where

- **method.md**: Building blocks across all workflows. Phases, knowledge state, convergence, collaboration model, how agents reason.
- **formation.md (Background)**: How the method developed. Nested under Method in nav. Brief — anecdote, productive friction, one paragraph of background. Not required reading.
- **quick-start.md**: Actionable techniques. No theory. Links back to full treatments. Alternative entry point.
- **agent-patterns.md**: Observed agent behavioral patterns — what they do, why, how the method works with them. Reference doc. Complements method.md.
- **workflows/**: How building blocks compose for specific types of work. Concrete. References method for principles. Lead with what's unique.
- **open-questions.md**: Living edges. Working doc, not published.

## Background-Specific

- **Brief.** Four paragraphs. Anecdote, productive friction, background, closing.
- **Lead with practice.** The locality bias anecdote is the opening.
- **Hameivin yavin intact.** "A field that rewarded the opposite of decomposition" — the unnamed tradition.
- **Acknowledge the self-fulfilling risk.** "Though the same training also makes me prone to finding connections."

## Calibrating Claims

- **Don't attribute common expert practice to the background.** Convergence-before-verification, gap recognition, batch feedback — experienced practitioners do these regardless of background.
- **Be honest about what you can't separate.**
- **Method stands alone.** The background illuminates but isn't prerequisite.

## Accumulated Feedback

- "Fractal" — stated once in method.md opening, shown through practice elsewhere. Don't restate or relabel ("self-similar") in other docs.
- Cold reads are one of the most valuable review perspectives. Spin up time-crunched cold readers alongside structural/editorial reviewers.
- Don't frame the method by contrast with other approaches — just state what it does.

## Audience

- **Primary (method + workflows + quick-start):** Technical practitioners using agentic tools. Senior. Not interested in epistemology.
- **Secondary (background):** Practitioners wanting the why. Cross-disciplinary backgrounds.
